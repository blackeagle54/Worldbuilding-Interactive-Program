# Celtic Mythology — Comprehensive Worldbuilding Reference Database

> **A Note on Sources**: Celtic mythology is significantly more fragmented than Norse. The Celts
> were an oral culture; their druids explicitly forbade writing down sacred knowledge. What
> survives comes from:
>
> - **Irish sources** (the richest): Medieval manuscripts written by Christian monks, including
>   the Lebor Gabala Erenn (Book of Invasions), the Mythological Cycle, the Ulster Cycle,
>   the Fenian Cycle, and the Cycle of Kings.
> - **Welsh sources**: The Mabinogion (a collection of prose tales from the 12th-13th century),
>   the poetry of Taliesin, and the Triads of the Island of Britain.
> - **Gaulish/Continental sources**: Roman accounts (Caesar, Lucan, Strabo), archaeological
>   evidence, inscriptions, and the Coligny Calendar.
> - **Brittonic and Scottish** traditions contribute additional material.
>
> Because the sources were recorded by Christians centuries after active worship ended, they
> are euhemerized — gods are often presented as ancient kings, heroes, or fairies rather than
> as deities. The mythology must be reconstructed, and scholars disagree on many points.
> This database presents the most widely accepted interpretations while noting uncertainties.

## 1. PANTHEON

### 1.1 The Tuatha De Danann (Irish — "Peoples of the Goddess Danu")
The Tuatha De Danann are the divine race of Irish mythology, later diminished into the sidhe (fairy folk) after their defeat by the Milesians (humans). They came to Ireland from four mythical cities — Falias, Gorias, Findias, and Murias — and brought four great treasures.

#### THE DAGDA (The Good God, Eochaid Ollathair)
- **Domain/Role**: Chief god (or one of the chiefs). God of agriculture, fertility, strength, druidry, weather, life and death. "Good" meaning "skilled at all things," not morally good.
- **Symbols**: His club (kills with one end, resurrects with the other), his cauldron (Coire Ansic — never empties, satisfies all hunger), his harp (Uaithne — controls the seasons and emotions).
- **Appearance**: Often depicted as large, crude, even comical — wearing a short tunic, dragging his enormous club on wheels.
- **Key Relationships**:
  - Father of Brigid, Aengus Og, Bodb Derg, Cermait, Midir, and others.
  - Lover of the Morrigan (they mated at Samhain before the Second Battle of Mag Tuired).
  - Lover of Boann (they conceived Aengus through magical manipulation of time).
- **Personality**: Earthy, powerful, generous, sometimes buffoonish. A figure of abundance.
- **Major Myths**: The Second Battle of Mag Tuired; his encounter with the Morrigan; the porridge of Fomorians (forced to eat a vast pit of porridge); fathering Aengus by making the sun stand still for nine months.

#### LUGH (Lugh Lamhfada — "Lugh of the Long Arm," Samildanach — "Master of All Arts")
- **Domain/Role**: God of skill, craft, warfare, oaths, truth, and the sun (possibly). The polymath deity — master of every art and craft simultaneously.
- **Symbols**: His spear (the Spear of Lugh, one of the four treasures), the raven, the lynx, the sling.
- **Key Relationships**:
  - Parents: Cian (Tuatha De Danann) and Ethniu (Fomorian — daughter of Balor).
  - Grandfather: Balor of the Evil Eye (whom he kills).
  - Foster-mother: Tailtiu (in whose honor he established the festival of Lughnasadh).
  - Son: Cuchulainn (by the mortal woman Deichtine).
- **Personality**: Brilliant, confident, charismatic. Arrived at the court of the Tuatha De Danann and was admitted only because no one person possessed all his skills combined.
- **Major Myths**: Arrival at Tara; leadership in the Second Battle of Mag Tuired; slaying Balor with a sling stone through his evil eye; establishing the Lughnasadh festival; possibly the divine father of Cuchulainn.
- **Gaulish Equivalent**: Lugus (attested in place names: Lyon, Leiden, Lugo).

#### THE MORRIGAN (also Morrigu, "Phantom Queen" or "Great Queen")
- **Domain/Role**: Goddess of war, fate, death, sovereignty, and prophecy. Often a triple goddess (the Morrigan, Badb, and Macha — or Nemain replacing one of these).
- **Symbols**: The crow/raven, the wolf, the eel, the cow.
- **Appearance**: Shape-shifter. Appears as a beautiful young woman, an old hag, a crow over battlefields, a wolf, an eel, or a red heifer.
- **Key Relationships**:
  - Lover of the Dagda (they mated before the Second Battle of Mag Tuired).
  - Attempted lover of Cuchulainn (he rejected her; she became his enemy).
- **Personality**: Terrifying, prophetic, sexually powerful, ambiguous. She does not fight in battles directly but influences their outcomes through magic, prophecy, and psychological warfare.
- **Major Myths**: The Second Battle of Mag Tuired (her prophecy); her encounters with Cuchulainn in the Tain Bo Cuailnge; her final prophecy of the end of the world.
- **Worldbuilding Note**: The Morrigan is NOT simply a "death goddess." She represents sovereignty — the land's right to choose its king. Her sexual encounters with rulers symbolize the land accepting or rejecting a king.

#### BRIGID (Brig, Brighid)
- **Domain/Role**: Goddess of poetry, healing, smithcraft, and the hearth fire. Possibly a triple goddess (three Brigids, each over one domain). Later syncretized with Saint Brigid of Kildare.
- **Symbols**: The flame, the well, the mantle/cloak, Brigid's cross.
- **Sacred Animals**: The cow, the swan, the serpent (spring awakening).
- **Key Relationships**:
  - Father: The Dagda.
  - Husband: Bres (the half-Fomorian who briefly ruled the Tuatha De Danann as a tyrant).
  - Son: Ruadan (killed in the Second Battle of Mag Tuired).
- **Personality**: Creative, nurturing, fierce in grief. Inventor of keening (the mourning wail) when her son Ruadan died.
- **Major Myths**: The Second Battle of Mag Tuired; the origin of keening; her association with Imbolc (the festival of spring).
- **Significance**: One of the most enduring Celtic deities — her worship survived into Christianity through Saint Brigid. Her eternal flame was kept burning at Kildare for centuries.

#### AENGUS OG (Aengus Mac Og, "Aengus the Young")
- **Domain/Role**: God of love, youth, poetry, and inspiration. Often called "the Young Son."
- **Symbols**: Four birds (said to be kisses) that circle his head. The harp.
- **Key Relationships**:
  - Parents: The Dagda and Boann (the river goddess, wife of Elcmar/Nechtan).
  - Lover/wife: Caer Ibormeith (a swan-maiden).
  - Foster-son: Diarmuid Ua Duibhne.
- **Personality**: Passionate, beautiful, clever. Tricked his father out of the Brug na Boinne (Newgrange) by asking to stay "a day and a night" — which in Irish means "all days and nights."
- **Major Myths**: The Dream of Aengus (searched all Ireland for a woman he saw in a dream — Caer Ibormeith, who alternated between human and swan form each Samhain; he transformed into a swan to be with her); his protection of Diarmuid and Grainne.

#### NUADA (Nuada Airgetlam — "Nuada of the Silver Hand/Arm")
- **Domain/Role**: King of the Tuatha De Danann. God of war and kingship. Associated with the sword (the Sword of Light of Nuada, one of the four treasures).
- **Key Relationships**: Lost his hand in the First Battle of Mag Tuired. Dian Cecht (the physician god) made him a silver one; later, Miach (Dian Cecht's son) grew him a real hand of flesh.
- **Major Myths**: Could not be king while maimed (sovereignty required physical perfection — an important Celtic concept). Temporarily replaced by Bres, who proved a tyrant. Regained kingship after his arm was restored.
- **Welsh Equivalent**: Nudd/Lludd Llaw Eraint ("Lludd of the Silver Hand").
- **Gaulish Equivalent**: Nodens.

#### MANANNÁN MAC LIR (Son of the Sea)
- **Domain/Role**: God of the sea, the Otherworld, weather, and magic. Guardian of the passage between worlds. Trickster figure.
- **Symbols**: The crane bag (containing magical treasures visible only at high tide), his horse Aonbarr (could ride over waves), his boat (the Wave Sweeper, needed no oars), his cloak of mist.
- **Key Relationships**:
  - Father: Lir (the sea).
  - Wife: Fand (who briefly loved Cuchulainn).
  - Foster-son: Lugh.
- **Personality**: Enigmatic, powerful, unpredictable. A liminal figure between worlds.
- **Major Myths**: Appears across many cycles. Rules Emain Ablach (the Isle of Apple Trees) and Tir Tairngire (the Land of Promise). Shakes his cloak between Cuchulainn and Fand to make them forget each other.
- **Welsh Equivalent**: Manawydan fab Llyr.

#### DIAN CECHT
- **Domain/Role**: God of medicine and healing.
- **Key Relationships**: Father of Miach, Airmed, and others.
- **Major Myths**: Created Nuada's silver hand. Killed his son Miach out of jealousy when Miach surpassed him by growing a real hand. Airmed (his daughter) catalogued all healing herbs that grew from Miach's grave, but Dian Cecht scattered them, so humans lost the knowledge.
- **Worldbuilding Note**: This myth explains why complete healing knowledge is lost — a common mythological motif.

#### GOIBNIU (Gaibhne)
- **Domain/Role**: God of smithcraft. One of the three craft-gods (Tri De Dana) along with Luchta (wright) and Credne (metalworker).
- **Major Myths**: Forged the weapons of the Tuatha De Danann for the Second Battle of Mag Tuired. Also brews the Fled Goibnenn (Feast of Goibniu) — an ale of immortality.

#### OGMA (Oghma)
- **Domain/Role**: God of eloquence, language, and strength. Champion of the Tuatha De Danann. Credited with inventing the Ogham alphabet.
- **Symbols**: Ogham script, the chain (Lucian describes a Gaulish Ogmios leading willing followers by golden chains from his tongue to their ears — eloquence binding men).
- **Major Myths**: Fought in the Second Battle of Mag Tuired. Captured the sword of the Fomorian king Tethra.
- **Gaulish Equivalent**: Ogmios.

#### BODB DERG
- **Domain/Role**: King of the Tuatha De Danann after the Dagda.
- **Key Relationships**: Son of the Dagda.
- **Major Myths**: Ruled the sidhe after the Tuatha De Danann withdrew underground. Appears in the tale of the Children of Lir.

#### MIDIR
- **Domain/Role**: A lord of the sidhe, associated with the underworld mound of Bri Leith.
- **Major Myths**: The Wooing of Etain — Midir's wife Etain was transformed into a fly by his jealous first wife Fuamnach, was blown about for seven years, fell into a cup of wine, was swallowed by a mortal woman and reborn as a human. Midir eventually won her back, and the High King Eochaid pursued them, leading to war.

#### LIR (the Elder)
- **Domain/Role**: The sea personified. Father of Manannan.
- **Note**: Often confused with his son. The "Children of Lir" are actually the grandchildren concept — Lir's human-form children by his wife Aobh.

#### CHILDREN OF LIR
- **Story**: Lir's four children (Fionnuala, Aodh, Fiachra, and Conn) were transformed into swans by their jealous stepmother Aoife for 900 years. One of the Three Sorrows of Storytelling.

### 1.2 The Fomorians (Fomoire)
- **Nature**: A supernatural race representing chaos, darkness, and the destructive forces of nature. Often depicted as monstrous (one-eyed, one-armed, one-legged). Associated with the sea and the undersea world.
- **Key Figures**:
  - **Balor of the Evil Eye**: Fomorian king/champion. His single eye, when opened, could destroy armies. Killed by his grandson Lugh with a sling stone. A classic "evil grandfather" motif.
  - **Bres**: Half-Fomorian, half-Tuatha De Danann. Beautiful but tyrannical. His failed kingship (he did not uphold the obligations of a king — no hospitality, no generosity) is a key sovereignty myth.
  - **Elatha**: Bres's Fomorian father. A beautiful golden figure who came from the sea.
  - **Domnu**: Possibly a Fomorian goddess of the abyss/deep.
- **Worldbuilding Note**: The Fomorians are NOT simply "evil." They are the necessary opposing force. Many Tuatha De Danann have Fomorian ancestry (Lugh, Bres). They represent nature's destructive side — storms, blight, sea-ravage — which must be balanced, not eliminated.

### 1.3 Welsh Deities and Mythological Figures

#### RHIANNON
- **Domain/Role**: Sovereignty goddess. Associated with horses, birds, patience, and the Otherworld.
- **Symbols**: White horse, three magical birds whose song could wake the dead and put the living to sleep.
- **Major Myths** (from the Mabinogion): Appeared on a magical white horse that no one could catch. Chose Pwyll as her husband. Was falsely accused of killing her infant son (Pryderi) and punished by carrying visitors on her back like a horse. Later married Manawydan.
- **Possible Cognate**: The Gaulish horse-goddess Epona.

#### ARAWN
- **Domain/Role**: King of Annwn (the Welsh Otherworld/underworld).
- **Major Myths**: Exchanged shapes with Pwyll for a year so Pwyll could defeat Arawn's rival Hafgan. Pwyll ruled Annwn so justly that he and Arawn became allies.

#### PWYLL
- **Domain/Role**: Prince of Dyfed. Called "Head of Annwn" after his year ruling the Otherworld.
- **Major Myths**: The First Branch of the Mabinogi — his year in Annwn, his wooing of Rhiannon, the disappearance of his son Pryderi.

#### PRYDERI
- **Domain/Role**: Son of Pwyll and Rhiannon. The only character who appears in all four branches of the Mabinogi.
- **Major Myths**: Stolen as an infant (found by Teyrnon in a stable beside a newborn foal). Grew preternaturally fast. Fought in the war in Ireland (Second Branch). Enchanted in Dyfed (Third Branch). Killed by Gwydion in the Fourth Branch.

#### BRAN THE BLESSED (Bendigeidfran)
- **Domain/Role**: A giant and King of Britain. His name means "Blessed Crow/Raven."
- **Major Myths** (Second Branch): Led an invasion of Ireland to rescue his sister Branwen. Was mortally wounded. Ordered his head cut off; it remained alive and talking for 87 years (the "Hospitality of the Noble Head"), entertaining his companions, before being buried in London facing France to protect Britain.
- **Possible Connection**: Later associated with the Fisher King of Arthurian legend.

#### BRANWEN
- **Domain/Role**: Sister of Bran. Her mistreatment in Ireland triggers a devastating war.
- **Major Myths**: Married the Irish king Matholwch but was abused and forced into servitude. Trained a starling to carry a message to Bran. The resulting war destroyed both nations. She died of grief.

#### GWYDION
- **Domain/Role**: Magician, trickster, and hero of the Fourth Branch of the Mabinogi. Associated with poetry and enchantment.
- **Key Relationships**: Son of Don (the Welsh mother-goddess figure). Brother of Arianrhod and Gilfaethwy.
- **Major Myths**: Engineered a war to steal pigs from Pryderi (using illusions). Helped his sister's son Lleu Llaw Gyffes overcome three curses. Shape-shifted as punishment.

#### ARIANRHOD
- **Domain/Role**: Goddess associated with the moon, fate, fertility, and the stars. Her fortress is Caer Arianrhod (the Corona Borealis constellation or a drowned land off the Welsh coast).
- **Major Myths**: Failed a virginity test by stepping over Math's magic wand, giving birth to Dylan (a sea-child) and a small form that Gwydion raised as Lleu. She cursed Lleu with three geasa: no name, no arms, and no human wife — all of which Gwydion circumvented.

#### LLEU LLAW GYFFES ("Bright One of the Skillful Hand")
- **Domain/Role**: A hero-figure and possible sun god. Welsh counterpart to the Irish Lugh.
- **Major Myths**: Born of Arianrhod, raised by Gwydion. Given a wife made of flowers (Blodeuwedd) by Math and Gwydion. Blodeuwedd and her lover Gronw plotted his death; he was transformed into an eagle, then restored by Gwydion.

#### BLODEUWEDD
- **Domain/Role**: A woman conjured from flowers (oak, broom, meadowsweet) by Math and Gwydion as a wife for Lleu.
- **Major Myths**: Fell in love with Gronw Pebr and plotted Lleu's death. Was punished by being transformed into an owl — forever shunned by other birds.
- **Worldbuilding Note**: Blodeuwedd's story raises the question of whether a created being has free will or the right to choose her own love.

#### MATH FAB MATHONWY
- **Domain/Role**: A king of Gwynedd and a powerful magician. Required a virgin footholder at all times (unless at war) or he would die.
- **Major Myths**: The Fourth Branch of the Mabinogi. Punished Gwydion and Gilfaethwy by transforming them into mated pairs of animals for three years.

#### DON
- **Domain/Role**: The Welsh mother-goddess. Equivalent to the Irish Danu. Mother of Gwydion, Arianrhod, Gilfaethwy, Amaethon, and Gofannon.

#### CERRIDWEN
- **Domain/Role**: A sorceress/goddess associated with the cauldron of poetic inspiration (Awen), transformation, and rebirth.
- **Major Myths**: Brewed a potion of inspiration for her ugly son Morfran. Her servant boy Gwion Bach accidentally received the three drops of inspiration, gained all knowledge, and fled — both he and Cerridwen shape-shifted through multiple forms (hare/greyhound, fish/otter, bird/hawk) until he became a grain of wheat and she, as a hen, swallowed him. She became pregnant and gave birth to him as the poet Taliesin.

#### TALIESIN
- **Domain/Role**: The greatest of bards. Possibly a historical poet (6th century) who became mythologized.
- **Major Myths**: Born from Cerridwen's cauldron (see above). Possesses all knowledge and prophetic ability. His poetry is among the oldest in Welsh.

### 1.4 Gaulish / Continental Celtic Deities
These are known primarily from Roman accounts, inscriptions, and interpretatio romana (Roman identification of foreign gods with their own).

| Deity | Domain | Notes |
|-------|--------|-------|

| **Cernunnos** | Wild animals, fertility, wealth, the underworld | The "Horned God." Depicted on the Gundestrup Cauldron: antlered, seated cross-legged, holding a torc and a serpent. No surviving myths — only iconography. |

## 2. COSMOLOGY

### 2.1 The Otherworld (Irish: An Saol Eile)
Celtic cosmology does not have a neat "nine worlds" structure like Norse. Instead, the Otherworld exists alongside and interpenetrating the mortal world. It can be accessed through:
- **Sidhe mounds** (fairy hills — the Tuatha De Danann retreated into these).
- **The sea** (Tir fo Thuinn — "Land Under the Wave"; Manannán's realm).
- **The western horizon** (Tir na nOg — "Land of the Young"; Hy-Brasil; Emain Ablach — "Isle of Apple Trees").
- **Lakes, wells, and springs** (liminal water spaces).
- **Mist and fog** (the boundary thins in mist).
- **The turning of the year** — especially Samhain (November 1) and Beltane (May 1), when the veil between worlds is thinnest.

### 2.2 Key Otherworld Realms

| Realm | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Tir na nOg** (Land of the Young) | An island paradise of eternal youth, beauty, and feasting. No sickness, no death, no aging. Time moves differently (centuries pass as days). |
| **Emain Ablach** (Isle of Apple Trees) | Manannán's domain. Apples of immortality. Parallels with Avalon. |
| **Mag Mell** (Plain of Delight) | A joyous realm accessible via sea voyage or invitation from a fairy woman. |
| **Tech Duinn** (House of Donn) | The gathering place of the dead. Donn, the first Milesian to die, presides. Located at Bull Rock island off the southwest coast of Ireland. |
| **Annwn** (Welsh) | The Otherworld ruled by Arawn. A land of abundance and feasting. Not a realm of punishment. Associated with the Cauldron of Rebirth. |
| **Caer Sidi / Caer Wydyr** | Otherworldly fortresses in Welsh tradition. Caer Sidi may be a revolving castle at the edge of the world. |

### 2.3 Cosmological Principles
- **No strict hierarchy of planes**: The Otherworld is not "above" or "below" — it is beside, within, and through. Walk through the right mist, enter the right mound, sail far enough west, and you are there.
- **Time dislocation**: A common motif. Mortals who visit the Otherworld return to find centuries have passed (Oisin in Tir na nOg) or very little time (Pwyll's year in Annwn).
- **The land is sacred and alive**: Sovereignty is not just political — it is spiritual. The land itself has a goddess (Eriu, Banba, Fodla for Ireland), and the king must be married to the land.
- **Circles and cycles**: The Celtic year is circular (the festivals mark a wheel). Time is not linear. The Otherworld is always present.
- **The importance of thresholds**: Doorways, boundaries, crossroads, riverbanks, shorelines, twilight, dawn — all are liminal and therefore magical.

## 3. CREATION MYTHS

### 3.1 Irish: The Book of Invasions (Lebor Gabala Erenn)
Celtic mythology lacks a single, clear cosmogonic creation myth like Norse. The closest is the Lebor Gabala Erenn, which describes the mythological history of Ireland as a series of invasions:

1. **Cessair**: The first settlers, led by a woman named Cessair (granddaughter of Noah, in the Christianized version). Almost all died in the Flood. One survivor, Fintan mac Bochra, transformed into a salmon, eagle, and hawk to survive for thousands of years.

2. **Partholon**: The first post-Flood settlers. They fought the Fomorians and introduced agriculture, brewing, and craftsmanship. All died of plague except Tuan mac Cairill, who survived by shape-shifting through multiple animal forms.

3. **Nemed**: The next wave. Also fought the Fomorians. The Fomorians imposed crushing tributes (two-thirds of their children, grain, and milk each Samhain). The Nemedians eventually rebelled, mostly died, and the survivors scattered — some to Greece (becoming the Fir Bolg), some to the north of the world (becoming the Tuatha De Danann).

4. **The Fir Bolg**: Returned to Ireland. Divided it into five provinces (the origin of Ireland's provinces). A peaceable people, less magical than what followed.

5. **The Tuatha De Danann**: Arrived "in dark clouds" (or on ships they burned upon landing). Defeated the Fir Bolg at the First Battle of Mag Tuired. Then defeated the Fomorians at the Second Battle of Mag Tuired. Ruled Ireland as gods.

6. **The Milesians (Sons of Mil)**: The final invaders — representing the Gaels, the mortal Irish. They defeated the Tuatha De Danann, who retreated into the sidhe mounds, becoming the fairy folk. Ireland was divided: the Milesians ruled the surface, the Tuatha De Danann ruled below.

### 3.2 The Deeper Creation
- There is no surviving Celtic myth describing the creation of the world itself (earth, sky, sea) from primordial material, as Norse mythology has with Ymir.
- Some scholars believe such myths existed but were suppressed or lost due to Christian recording.
- The Dindshenchas (lore of place-names) contains fragments suggesting the land was shaped by divine actions — rivers created by goddesses (Boann formed the River Boyne by approaching a forbidden well; Sinann formed the Shannon similarly).
- The Welsh poem "Cad Goddeu" (The Battle of the Trees) describes Gwydion animating trees as warriors — possibly a fragment of a creation or transformation myth.

### 3.3 Welsh Cosmogony
- No surviving creation myth per se. The Mabinogion begins with the world already existing.
- The poem "Preiddeu Annwn" (The Spoils of Annwn) describes Arthur raiding the Otherworld — possibly containing cosmological fragments.

## 4. MAJOR MYTHS AND STORY CYCLES

### 4.1 The Mythological Cycle (Irish)
The tales of the Tuatha De Danann, including:
- **Cath Maige Tuired** (The Battle of Mag Tuired): The central myth. The Tuatha De Danann versus the Fomorians. Lugh's arrival at Tara, the tyranny of Bres, the battle itself (featuring magical weapons, healing wells, and the Dagda's encounter with the Morrigan), Lugh's slaying of Balor, and the Morrigan's prophecy.
- **The Wooing of Etain** (Tochmarc Etaine): Midir's love for Etain, her transformation into a fly, rebirth as a mortal, and Midir winning her back through a chess game — he kisses her and both fly out through the roof as swans.
- **The Dream of Aengus** (Aislinge Oenguso): Aengus searches for the woman of his dreams, Caer Ibormeith, who transforms into a swan each Samhain. He becomes a swan and they fly to the Brug na Boinne together, their song putting all who hear it to sleep for three days.
- **The Children of Lir** (Oidhe Chlainne Lir): Four children transformed into swans for 900 years. One of the Three Sorrows.
- **The Fate of the Children of Tuireann** (Oidhe Chloinne Tuireann): The sons of Tuireann kill Lugh's father Cian. Lugh demands impossible quests as blood-price. They complete them all but die from their wounds. One of the Three Sorrows.

### 4.2 The Ulster Cycle
Set in the time of the hero Cuchulainn and the warriors of Ulster. Centered on the conflict between Ulster and Connacht.

- **Tain Bo Cuailnge** (The Cattle Raid of Cooley): The central epic. Queen Medb of Connacht invades Ulster to seize the great bull Donn Cuailnge. The men of Ulster are struck by a curse (the cess noidhen — they experience labor pains at their time of greatest need, a curse from the goddess Macha). The teenage Cuchulainn alone defends Ulster, fighting single combats at a ford. He kills his foster-brother Ferdiad in the most tragic duel in the cycle. The bulls eventually fight; both die.
- **The Boyhood Deeds of Cuchulainn**: As a child named Setanta, he killed the hound of Culann with his bare hands and offered to serve as the new guard dog — hence "Cu Chulainn" (Hound of Culann).
- **The Death of Cuchulainn** (Aided Con Culainn): Bound by conflicting geasa (taboos), Cuchulainn is tricked into breaking them one by one. Mortally wounded, he ties himself to a standing stone so he can die on his feet. A crow (the Morrigan) lands on his shoulder, signaling his death.
- **The Exile of the Sons of Uisneach** (Longes mac nUislenn): Deirdre, prophesied at birth to cause great sorrow, falls in love with Naoise and flees with him to Scotland. King Conchobar lures them back with false promises, kills Naoise, and Deirdre dies of grief (or kills herself). One of the Three Sorrows.
- **The Intoxication of the Ulstermen** (Mesca Ulad): The Ulstermen get drunk and wander accidentally into enemy territory.

### 4.3 The Fenian Cycle (Ossianic Cycle)
Set later than the Ulster Cycle. Centered on Fionn mac Cumhaill and his war band, the Fianna.

- **The Boyhood of Fionn**: Fionn (Finn) gained all knowledge by accidentally tasting the Salmon of Knowledge while cooking it for his master, the druid Finnegas. He burned his thumb on the salmon and sucked it — gaining wisdom. Ever after, he could access knowledge by putting his thumb in his mouth.
- **Fionn and the Fianna**: The Fianna were an elite warrior band who lived in the wilderness, serving the High King. Admission required passing physical tests and literary/poetic tests — a warrior-poet ideal.
- **The Pursuit of Diarmuid and Grainne** (Toraigheacht Dhiarmada agus Ghrainne): Grainne, betrothed to the aging Fionn, placed a geis (magical compulsion) on the young warrior Diarmuid to elope with her. They fled across Ireland, pursued by Fionn. Eventually reconciled, but Fionn arranged Diarmuid's death through a boar hunt (Diarmuid's geis forbade him from hunting boar). Fionn had healing water but deliberately let it slip through his fingers. Prototype of the Tristan and Isolde story.
- **Oisin in Tir na nOg**: Fionn's son Oisin (Ossian) fell in love with Niamh of the Golden Hair, who took him to Tir na nOg. He lived there in bliss for what seemed three years but was 300. He returned to Ireland, was warned never to touch the ground, but fell from his horse and instantly aged into a blind old man. He met Saint Patrick and debated with him — the old pagan world meeting the new Christian one.

### 4.4 The Four Branches of the Mabinogi (Welsh)
The core of Welsh mythology:

- **First Branch: Pwyll, Prince of Dyfed** — Pwyll's year in Annwn; his wooing of Rhiannon; the birth and disappearance of Pryderi.
- **Second Branch: Branwen, Daughter of Llyr** — Branwen's marriage to the Irish king; her mistreatment; Bran's invasion of Ireland; the Cauldron of Rebirth; the destruction of both nations; Bran's living head.
- **Third Branch: Manawydan, Son of Llyr** — Manawydan and Rhiannon's marriage; an enchantment empties Dyfed; Manawydan uses craft and cunning (not violence) to break the spell.
- **Fourth Branch: Math, Son of Mathonwy** — Gwydion and Gilfaethwy's crimes; their punishment; the birth of Lleu; Blodeuwedd; Lleu's near-death and restoration.

### 4.5 Arthurian Connections
The earliest Arthurian material is Welsh/Celtic, not French:
- **Culhwch and Olwen**: An early Welsh tale where Arthur helps his cousin complete impossible tasks to win the giant's daughter.
- **Preiddeu Annwn**: Arthur raids the Otherworld for a magical cauldron (prototype of the Grail quest).
- Arthur himself may have originated as a Celtic war-god or culture hero before becoming the chivalric king of later French romance.

## 5. CREATURES AND MONSTERS

### 5.1 Fairy Folk and Supernatural Beings

| Creature | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Sidhe (Aos Si)** | The Tuatha De Danann after retreating into the mounds. Beautiful, powerful, dangerous. Not the small fairies of later tradition — tall, shining, and terrifying. |
| **Bean Sidhe (Banshee)** | A fairy woman whose wailing (keening) foretells death, particularly of old Irish families. Not malevolent — a herald of fate. |
| **Leprechaun** | A fairy cobbler. In original mythology, a small, solitary craftsman. The pot-of-gold cliche is a later development. |
| **Pooka (Puca)** | A shape-shifting trickster, often appearing as a dark horse, goat, or hare. Can be helpful or harmful. |
| **Dullahan** | A headless rider on a black horse, carrying its own head. When it speaks your name, you die. May crack a whip made of a human spine. |
| **Changeling** | A fairy child swapped for a human baby. The fairy child is sickly and demanding. Reflects real anxieties about infant illness. |
| **Fear Gorta** | "Man of Hunger." A phantom of famine. Appears as an emaciated figure begging for food. Those who give are blessed; those who refuse are cursed. |
| **Merrow** | Irish merfolk. Female merrows are beautiful; male merrows are ugly. They wear red caps (cohuleen druith) to travel underwater. |
| **Selkie** | Seal-folk who can shed their skins to become human. If their skin is stolen, they are trapped on land. A common story involves a fisherman hiding a selkie woman's skin. |
| **Each-Uisge** | A deadly water horse (Scottish/Irish). Appears as a beautiful horse; if mounted, it dives into the water and devours the rider (except the liver, which floats to shore). |
| **Kelpie** | Scottish water horse, similar to each-uisge but associated with rivers rather than lochs. |

### 5.2 Welsh and Broader Celtic Creatures

| Creature | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Cwn Annwn** | The hounds of Annwn — white with red ears. They hunt the souls of the dead. Their baying sounds loud when far away and soft when close. |
| **Afanc** | A Welsh lake monster — a giant beaver or crocodile-like creature dragged from a lake by oxen (or by the maiden who lured it to sleep in her lap). |
| **Cath Palug** | A monstrous cat in Welsh and French Arthurian tradition, raised on Anglesey, eventually killed by (or killing) Arthur or Cei. |
| **Twrch Trwyth** | A king transformed into a monstrous boar, hunted by Arthur across Wales and Ireland in the tale of Culhwch and Olwen. Carries magical treasures between its ears. |
| **Coraniaid** | Beings who could hear every whisper carried on the wind. One of the three plagues of Britain in the tale of Lludd and Llefelys. |
| **Ellyllon** | Welsh elves/fairies. Tiny, associated with toadstools and foxglove. |

### 5.3 Monsters and Adversaries

| Creature | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Balor's Evil Eye** | Not a creature per se, but Balor's eye was so destructive it required four men to lift its lid. Its gaze destroyed everything before it. |
| **Aillen (Aillén mac Midgna)** | A fire-breathing fairy who burned Tara every Samhain after putting the defenders to sleep with music. Fionn killed him by staying awake (pressing a magical spear to his forehead). |
| **Fomorian monsters** | Various monstrous beings among the Fomorians — one-legged, one-armed figures, sea-demons, blight-bringers. |
| **The Grey of Macha and Black of Sainglend** | Cuchulainn's two chariot horses. The Grey of Macha wept tears of blood foreseeing Cuchulainn's death. Supernatural war-horses, not monsters, but significant. |

## 6. HEROES AND MORTAL FIGURES

### 6.1 Cuchulainn (Cu Chulainn, the Hound of Culann)
- **Nature**: The greatest hero of the Ulster Cycle. Son of Deichtine (a mortal) and Lugh (or Sualtam, her mortal husband). A demigod.
- **Abilities**: The warp-spasm (riastrad) — a terrifying battle-frenzy that transforms his body into a monstrous, nearly invincible shape. Master of the gae bolga (a barbed spear that opens thirty wounds inside the body).
- **Geasa (taboos)**: Must never eat dog meat (he is the "Hound"); must never refuse hospitality. His enemies exploited the conflict between these.
- **Deeds**: Defended Ulster single-handedly during the Tain; killed Ferdiad (his foster-brother) at the ford; fought the Morrigan (in her animal forms) and later accepted her healing.
- **Death**: Bound himself to a standing stone to die upright. His enemies only knew he was dead when the Morrigan, as a crow, landed on his shoulder.
- **Significance**: The archetypal Celtic hero — young, brilliant, doomed. His life is a meditation on the cost of glory.

### 6.2 Fionn mac Cumhaill (Finn McCool)
- **Nature**: Leader of the Fianna. A warrior, hunter, poet, and seer.
- **Abilities**: Gained all knowledge from the Salmon of Knowledge. Could access it by sucking his thumb.
- **Deeds**: Killed Aillen the fire-breather; led the Fianna across Ireland; numerous hunts, battles, and adventures.
- **Character**: In youth, noble and generous. In age, jealous and petty (his treatment of Diarmuid).
- **Significance**: A folk hero who remains in popular Irish culture. Some legends say he never died but sleeps in a cave, waiting to be called when Ireland needs him.

### 6.3 Oisin (Ossian)
- **Nature**: Son of Fionn. Poet and warrior.
- **Significance**: His journey to Tir na nOg and his return to find the world changed is one of the most poignant tales in Irish mythology — the old world confronting the new.

### 6.4 Diarmuid Ua Duibhne
- **Nature**: The most handsome of the Fianna. Had a love-spot on his forehead — any woman who saw it fell in love with him.
- **Death**: Killed by a magical boar (his geis). Fionn could have saved him but chose not to.

### 6.5 Medb (Maeve)
- **Nature**: Queen of Connacht. Not merely a human queen — likely a sovereignty goddess euhemerized into a mortal.
- **Character**: Proud, fierce, sexually independent (she said her husband must be "without jealousy, fear, or meanness"). She launched the Tain because she wanted to equal her husband in wealth.
- **Significance**: One of the most complex female figures in any mythology. Neither villain nor hero — a force of nature.

### 6.6 Conchobar mac Nessa
- **Nature**: King of Ulster. Patron of Cuchulainn.
- **Character**: Wise but treacherous. His betrayal of Deirdre and the sons of Uisneach is one of his darkest acts.

### 6.7 Deirdre of the Sorrows
- **Nature**: Prophesied at birth to bring ruin. The most beautiful woman in Ireland.
- **Fate**: Fled with Naoise to escape Conchobar. Lured back, Naoise killed. Deirdre killed herself (some versions: she dashed her head against a rock from Conchobar's chariot rather than live as his captive).
- **Significance**: One of the Three Sorrows of Storytelling. An archetype of tragic beauty.

## 7. THE AFTERLIFE

### 7.1 Core Concept
The Celtic afterlife is radically different from the Norse (and later Christian) model. There is no clear moral judgment — no "heaven" for the good and "hell" for the bad. The Otherworld is a place of continuity, feasting, youth, and beauty.

### 7.2 Tech Duinn (House of Donn)
- **Nature**: The gathering place of the dead, located at Bull Rock island off southwest Ireland.
- **Ruler**: Donn, the first of the Milesians to die. He is a lord of the dead, not a punisher.
- **Function**: A waystation or assembly point. The dead may pass through here on their way elsewhere.
- **Worldbuilding Note**: This is the closest thing to a "universal" afterlife destination in Irish mythology.

### 7.3 Tir na nOg and the Blessed Isles
- **Nature**: A paradise of eternal youth. No sickness, no aging, no sorrow. Feasting, beauty, music, and love.
- **Who Goes There**: Those who are invited or who find their way — often heroes or those loved by fairy women.
- **Time**: Flows differently. Centuries in the mortal world are days in Tir na nOg.
- **Risk**: Returning to the mortal world means instantly aging to your true chronological age.

### 7.4 Reincarnation / Transmigration
- Classical authors (Caesar, Diodorus Siculus) report that the Celts believed in the transmigration of souls — that the soul passes from one body to another after death.
- This is supported by mythological evidence: Tuan mac Cairill and Fintan mac Bochra survive through multiple animal and human incarnations. Etain is reborn as a mortal.
- The exact nature of this belief is debated, but a cyclical view of existence is deeply embedded in Celtic thought.

### 7.5 Annwn (Welsh)
- **Nature**: The Welsh Otherworld. A land of abundance and joy, not punishment.
- **Ruler**: Arawn.
- **Features**: Contains the Cauldron of Rebirth (which can bring dead warriors back to life, though they cannot speak). Hunted by the Cwn Annwn.
- **Access**: Through certain hills, lakes, or by sailing.

### 7.6 What Celtic Mythology Does NOT Have
- A hell or place of punishment for the wicked (this is a later Christian overlay).
- A clear moral sorting system at death.
- A permanent, irreversible death — the boundary between life and death is permeable.

## 8. SACRED PLACES

### 8.1 Irish Sacred Sites

| Place | Significance |
|-------|-------------|
| **Tara (Teamhair)** | Seat of the High Kings of Ireland. The Stone of Fal (Lia Fail) screams when the rightful king touches it. The spiritual center of Ireland. |
| **Newgrange (Brug na Boinne)** | A Neolithic passage tomb older than the pyramids. Home of the Dagda, then Aengus. The winter solstice sunrise illuminates the inner chamber — a literal intersection of astronomy and mythology. |
| **Emain Macha (Navan Fort)** | Capital of Ulster. Court of Conchobar. The primary setting of the Ulster Cycle. Named for the goddess Macha. |
| **Cruachan (Rathcroghan)** | Capital of Connacht. Court of Medb. Contains the Cave of Cruachan (Oweynagat) — a portal to the Otherworld from which monsters emerge on Samhain. |
| **Tailteann (Teltown)** | Where Lugh established the festival of Lughnasadh in honor of his foster-mother Tailtiu. Site of athletic games. |
| **Uisneach** | The "navel of Ireland." Where the five provinces meet. Sacred fire site. A stone called Ail na Mireann (the Stone of Divisions) marks the spot. |
| **Lough Derg** | Associated with Otherworld entrances and later Saint Patrick's Purgatory. |
| **Ben Bulben** | Mountain in Sligo where Diarmuid was killed by the boar. |
| **The Boyne River** | Sacred river created by the goddess Boann. The Boyne Valley is the mythological heartland of Ireland. |

### 8.2 Welsh and Broader Celtic Sites

| Place | Significance |
|-------|-------------|
| **Anglesey (Ynys Mon)** | Center of druidic learning and worship. The Romans under Suetonius Paulinus attacked it in 60 CE — Tacitus describes druids cursing from the shore. |
| **Glastonbury Tor** | Possibly an entrance to Annwn. Later associated with Avalon. |
| **The Source of the Seine** | Temple of the goddess Sequana. Pilgrims left wooden and stone votive offerings. |
| **Nemeton sites** | "Sacred groves." Found across the Celtic world. The term appears in place names (Drunemeton, Medionemeton, Nemetobriga). |

## 10. CULTURAL PRACTICES

### 10.1 The Druids
- **Role**: Priests, judges, teachers, advisors, astronomers, and keepers of sacred knowledge. The intellectual elite of Celtic society.
- **Training**: Reportedly 20 years of memorization. All knowledge was oral — writing sacred texts was forbidden.
- **Powers (attributed)**: Prophecy, weather magic, shape-shifting, geasa (laying magical prohibitions), satire (a druid's satire could raise boils and cause death or disgrace).
- **Sacrifice**: Classical sources (Caesar, Strabo) describe human sacrifice, including the burning of wicker men. Archaeological evidence partially supports this (bog bodies). How widespread or common this was is debated.
- **Status**: Druids could speak before kings. They had legal and spiritual authority.

### 10.2 The Bards (Filid)
- **Role**: Poets, historians, genealogists, and satirists. In Ireland, the filid (singular: fili) were a professional class with legal status.
- **Power**: A poet's satire was feared as much as a warrior's sword. A justified satire could strip a king of his sovereignty. An unjust satire would rebound on the poet.
- **Training**: Like druids, years of study and memorization. Mastery of complex poetic forms.
- **Seven Grades of Filid**: From fochloc (beginner) to ollam (master — equal in status to a king).

### 10.3 Festivals (the Celtic Year)
The Celtic year was divided by four major festivals, each marking a pastoral/agricultural turning point:

| Festival | Date | Significance |
|----------|------|-------------|
| **Samhain** | November 1 (begins at sunset October 31) | The Celtic New Year. The boundary between worlds thins. The dead walk. The sidhe mounds open. Great assemblies, feasts, and legal proceedings at Tara. Cattle brought in from pasture. The great darkness begins. |
| **Imbolc** | February 1 | Associated with Brigid. The first stirring of spring. Ewes begin to lactate. Purification rites. Brigid's crosses woven. |
| **Beltane** | May 1 | The bright half of the year begins. Great bonfires lit (cattle driven between them for purification). Fertility celebrations. The Otherworld veil thins again. |
| **Lughnasadh** | August 1 | Established by Lugh for his foster-mother Tailtiu. Harvest festival. Athletic games, horse racing, legal contracts, matchmaking. |

### 10.4 Geasa (Taboos)
- **Nature**: A geis (plural: geasa) is a magical prohibition or obligation placed on an individual, especially heroes and kings. Breaking a geis brings disaster; being forced to break one through conflicting geasa is a common tragic device.
- **Examples**: Cuchulainn could not eat dog meat (he was the "Hound") or refuse hospitality. When offered dog meat by an old woman, both geasa conflicted — he ate it and began to weaken.
- **Worldbuilding Note**: Geasa are one of the most distinctive and useful Celtic concepts for worldbuilders. They create inevitable tragedy and complex moral dilemmas without requiring a "villain."

### 10.5 Sovereignty and Kingship
- **The King and the Land**: The king is metaphysically married to the land-goddess. A just king brings prosperity (good harvests, peace, fair weather). An unjust king brings blight, famine, and defeat.
- **Physical Perfection**: A king must be physically unblemished (Nuada lost his throne when he lost his hand). This extends to symbolic perfection — a king must be generous, brave, truthful, and just.
- **The Sovereignty Goddess**: Appears as an ugly hag who transforms into a beautiful woman when the rightful king kisses (or sleeps with) her. A test of worthiness.
- **Inauguration**: Involved rituals at sacred sites. The Lia Fail screams for the rightful king. In some traditions, the king slept with or symbolically mated with a white mare (Giraldus Cambrensis describes such a ritual, though this account is controversial).

### 10.6 Ogham Script
- **Nature**: An alphabet of lines and notches carved on stone (and presumably wood). Each letter is named after a tree or plant.
- **Attributed Creator**: Ogma of the Tuatha De Danann.
- **Use**: Boundary markers, memorials, possibly divination. The Ogham tract associates each letter with a tree, a color, a bird, and other correspondences — a system ripe for magical use.

### 10.7 Head Veneration
- The Celts revered the human head as the seat of the soul. Classical sources and archaeology confirm:
  - Heads of enemies were collected as trophies.
  - Skulls were displayed at temples and hill-forts.
  - In mythology: Bran's living, talking head protects Britain for 87 years. Conall Cernach collected heads.
- **Worldbuilding Note**: This is a genuinely distinctive Celtic practice that can be developed for worldbuilding — a culture where the head is the locus of spiritual power.

### 10.8 Sacred Waters and Votive Offerings
- Rivers, lakes, springs, and bogs were liminal spaces — gateways to the Otherworld.
- Valuable objects (swords, shields, jewelry, cauldrons) were deliberately deposited in water as offerings.
- Bog bodies (Lindow Man, Tollund Man, Old Croghan Man) may be ritual sacrifices or offerings.
- Many Celtic deities are associated with specific rivers or springs (Boann = Boyne, Sequana = Seine, Sinann = Shannon, Sulis = the springs at Bath).

## 11. THEMES AND WORLDBUILDING LESSONS

### 11.1 The Sovereignty of the Land
Celtic mythology is inseparable from the landscape. Every hill, river, and plain has a story. The land is not a backdrop — it is a character, a goddess, and a source of power. **Worldbuilding lesson**: Tie your mythology to specific geography. Name every river after a god. Let the land have opinions about who rules it.

### 11.2 The Permeability of Boundaries
In Celtic mythology, the wall between worlds is thin and porous. The dead are not far away. The Otherworld is one mist-step from the mortal world. Time is not fixed. **Lesson**: A world where boundaries are soft creates a fundamentally different kind of story than one with hard metaphysical walls.

### 11.3 Obligations Over Morality
Celtic heroes are not destroyed by "evil" — they are destroyed by conflicting obligations (geasa). Cuchulainn does not fall because he is bad; he falls because the taboos placed on him are made to conflict. **Lesson**: Build your world's tragic engine on obligations, taboos, and conflicting duties rather than simple good-vs-evil.

### 11.4 The Non-Judgmental Afterlife
The Celtic Otherworld is not a reward or a punishment — it simply is. This creates a fundamentally different relationship with death than religions with moral afterlives. **Lesson**: Consider what it means for your world if death is not feared as punishment but as a transition to a different, perhaps richer existence.

### 11.5 The Power of Words
In Celtic culture, a poet's words could literally wound, kill, or heal. Satire was a weapon. A king's truth sustained the land. Words were not representations of power — they were power itself. **Lesson**: Make language, naming, and poetry genuinely magical in your world, not just decoration.

### 11.6 The Triple Pattern
Threes pervade Celtic mythology: three Brigids, three Morrigans, three Sorrows, three treasures (or four), three-fold deaths, triads as a literary form. **Lesson**: Use consistent numerical patterns to create a sense of underlying cosmic order.

### 11.7 Euhemerization as a Worldbuilding Tool
The gods of the Tuatha De Danann became the sidhe — fairies. A once-divine race diminished over time into folklore. **Lesson**: Consider what happens to your world's gods over deep time. Do they fade? Shrink? Become legends that villagers half-remember? This creates rich layering.

### 11.8 The Importance of Hospitality
Hospitality is sacred and obligatory. Refusing hospitality or being a poor host has devastating consequences (Bres lost kingship for poor hospitality). Cuchulainn died partly because he could not refuse offered food. **Lesson**: Make hospitality laws real and consequential in your world.

### 11.9 Women as Agents of Sovereignty
Celtic mythology is not feminist in a modern sense, but women hold genuine power: Medb commands armies and chooses her own lovers; the Morrigan determines who wins battles; Rhiannon chooses her own husband; sovereignty is literally a goddess. **Lesson**: Even in patriarchal worlds, embed genuine female power in the mythological structure.

### 11.10 Fragmentation as Richness
Celtic mythology is incomplete. There are gaps, contradictions, and mysteries. And that makes it more evocative, not less. **Lesson**: You do not need to explain everything in your world. Gaps and mysteries make mythology feel real. Real-world mythologies are always fragmentary — yours should be too.

### 11.11 The Hero's Inevitable Doom
The greatest Celtic heroes (Cuchulainn, Diarmuid, Deirdre, Ferdiad) are defined by their tragic fates. Glory and doom are inseparable. **Lesson**: A mythology where heroism always ends in loss creates a bittersweet, mature tone.

## APPENDIX: QUICK REFERENCE FOR WORLDBUILDERS

### Naming Conventions
- Irish names: patronymics (mac = son of, ni/ingen = daughter of). Epithets are common: Lugh Lamhfada ("Long-Armed"), Cuchulainn ("Hound of Culann"), Nuada Airgetlam ("Silver-Handed").
- Welsh names: "ap" or "fab/mab" = son of. Math fab Mathonwy, Pryderi fab Pwyll.
- Place names: dun = fort, rath = ring-fort, lough = lake, mag/magh = plain, ben = peak, inis = island, tir = land.

### Power Structure
- Irish: High King at Tara, five provincial kings beneath. Druids advise but can override. Poets wield legal and magical authority. Warriors form war-bands (fiana).
- Welsh: Kingdoms with lords (Math, Pwyll). Magicians hold outsized influence (Gwydion).
- The relationship between kings, druids, and warriors is triangular, not simply hierarchical.

### Tone
- Deeply emotional. Characters weep, rage, love, and grieve openly. Beauty and sorrow coexist on every page. Violence is vivid but not glorified in isolation — it carries weight and consequence. Humor appears (the Intoxication of the Ulstermen is essentially a comedy). The sense of loss is profound — of youth, of the old world, of love.

### What Celtic Mythology Does NOT Have
- A single coherent cosmogony (creation from void/chaos, as in Norse or Greek).
- A clear apocalypse/Ragnarok equivalent (the Morrigan prophesies a dark future, but there is no single world-ending battle).
- Absolute good vs. absolute evil (Fomorians are adversaries, not incarnations of evil; many gods have Fomorian blood).
- Fixed, systematic afterlife sorting.
- A single authoritative text (unlike the Eddas or the Bible — Celtic mythology must be pieced together from many fragmentary sources).

### Key Differences from Norse (for Comparative Worldbuilding)
| Feature | Norse | Celtic |
|---------|-------|--------|
| Creation | Detailed (Ginnungagap, Ymir) | Fragmentary / absent |
| Apocalypse | Ragnarok (specific, prophesied) | Vague prophecy of decline |
| Afterlife | Structured (Valhalla, Hel, etc.) | Fluid (Otherworld, Tir na nOg) |
| Cosmology | Vertical (World Tree, Nine Worlds) | Horizontal (Otherworld beside/within) |
| Gods' fate | Doomed, mortal at Ragnarok | Diminished into sidhe, not killed |
| Hero type | Warrior-fate (die well) | Warrior-poet (live brilliantly, die tragically) |
| Magic system | Runes, seidr, galdr | Geasa, druidry, satire, shape-shifting |
| Primary tension | Order vs. Chaos | Obligation vs. Obligation |
| Landscape | Cosmic (realms on a tree) | Local (every hill and river is sacred) |
