## 1. PANTHEON / MAJOR FIGURES

### 1A. GOD (YHWH / The Lord)

- **Names and Titles:** YHWH (the Tetragrammaton, the personal name revealed to Moses),
  Elohim (plural form — "gods" used for the one God), El Shaddai (God Almighty),
  Adonai (Lord), El Elyon (God Most High), the Alpha and Omega, the Ancient of Days,
  I AM THAT I AM (Ehyeh Asher Ehyeh)
- **Nature:** Singular, omnipotent, omniscient, omnipresent. Creator of all things.
  Exists outside of and before creation. No physical form (though manifests physically
  in theophanies). Both wrathful and merciful. Jealous (demands exclusive worship).
- **Key Traits:** Just, holy, sovereign, loving, wrathful against sin, faithful to
  covenants, unpredictable (the Book of Job), creative, linguistic (creates by speaking)
- **Manifestations:**
  - The Burning Bush (Exodus 3) — fire that does not consume
  - The Pillar of Cloud by day and Pillar of Fire by night (Exodus 13)
  - The Still Small Voice (1 Kings 19) — after earthquake, wind, and fire
  - The Glory (Kavod/Shekinah) — a radiant, overwhelming divine presence that fills
    the Tabernacle and Temple
  - The Whirlwind (Job 38) — speaks from the storm to silence Job's questions
- **Worldbuilding Note:** The Biblical God is unusual among mythological supreme beings
  in being genuinely singular and having no consort, rival, or equal. His power is
  absolute, which means all dramatic tension must come from the relationship between
  this absolute power and the free will of created beings. This is a fundamentally
  different narrative engine than polytheistic pantheons.

### 1B. THE ANGELIC HIERARCHY

Angels in Biblical and apocryphal tradition form a structured celestial court.

#### MICHAEL — The Warrior Archangel
- **Role:** Commander of the heavenly armies. Guardian of Israel. Combatant against
  Satan. The angel who fights the dragon in Revelation 12.
- **Key Stories:**
  - Led the war against Lucifer and the rebel angels, casting them out of Heaven
  - Disputed with Satan over the body of Moses (Jude 9)
  - Stands guard over Israel in the end times (Daniel 12:1)
- **Symbols:** Sword, armor, scales of justice, dragon underfoot
- **Character:** Fierce, loyal, militant, obedient to God without question

#### GABRIEL — The Messenger Archangel
- **Role:** God's primary herald and announcer. Delivers the most important divine
  messages in the Biblical narrative.
- **Key Stories:**
  - Interpreted Daniel's visions (Daniel 8-9)
  - Announced the birth of John the Baptist to Zechariah (Luke 1)
  - Announced the birth of Jesus to Mary (the Annunciation — Luke 1:26-38)
  - In Islamic tradition (Jibril), delivered the Quran to Muhammad
- **Symbols:** Trumpet (tradition), lily (in art), scroll
- **Character:** Authoritative, awe-inspiring, reassuring ("Fear not")

#### RAPHAEL — The Healer Archangel (Apocryphal)
- **Role:** Healing, guidance, protection of travelers
- **Key Stories:**
  - Central figure in the Book of Tobit (deuterocanonical). Disguises himself as a
    human traveler named "Azarias" and guides young Tobias on a journey. Teaches him
    to use fish organs to cure his father's blindness and to drive away the demon
    Asmodeus. Reveals his true identity at the end.
- **Symbols:** Staff, fish, traveler's garb
- **Character:** Warm, practical, protective, disguised among mortals

#### LUCIFER / SATAN — The Adversary
- **Names:** Lucifer ("Light-Bearer"), Satan ("Adversary"), the Devil, Beelzebub
  ("Lord of the Flies"), the Dragon, the Serpent, the Morning Star, Prince of Darkness
- **Origin (Composite tradition):**
  - Isaiah 14:12 — "How you have fallen from heaven, morning star, son of the dawn"
    (originally about the King of Babylon, later reinterpreted as Satan's fall)
  - Ezekiel 28 — A guardian cherub, perfect in beauty, set on the holy mountain of God,
    who fell through pride (originally about the King of Tyre)
  - Revelation 12 — The great dragon swept a third of the stars (angels) from heaven
  - In Job, Satan is a member of God's court — "the accuser" who tests Job with God's
    permission. He is not yet the cosmic adversary of later tradition.
  - In the Garden of Eden (Genesis 3), the Serpent tempts Eve. The identification of
    the Serpent with Satan comes from later tradition, not Genesis itself.
- **Composite Profile:** The most beautiful and powerful of all angels. Fell through
  pride — the desire to be equal to or greater than God. Became the ruler of the fallen
  angels (demons). Tempter of humanity. Adversary of God's plan. Will be defeated at
  the end of time.
- **Key Traits:** Intelligent, persuasive, proud, deceptive, ambitious, charismatic
- **Worldbuilding Note:** Satan is a composite figure assembled across centuries of
  texts. His characterization evolved from "heavenly prosecutor" (Job) to "cosmic
  adversary" (Revelation). This evolution itself is a worldbuilding lesson — mythological
  figures gain depth through accumulation and reinterpretation.

#### OTHER ANGELIC BEINGS

- **Seraphim:** Six-winged beings surrounding God's throne (Isaiah 6). Two wings cover
  their faces, two cover their feet, two are used for flight. They cry "Holy, Holy,
  Holy" continuously. Their name means "burning ones."
- **Cherubim:** NOT the chubby babies of Renaissance art. Described as powerful,
  terrifying guardians with multiple faces (human, lion, ox, eagle) and multiple wings.
  Guard the entrance to Eden with a flaming sword (Genesis 3:24). Form God's throne/
  chariot in Ezekiel's vision.
- **Ophanim (Thrones/Wheels):** From Ezekiel 1 — wheels within wheels, covered in
  eyes, moving in any direction without turning. One of the most alien and unsettling
  images in Biblical literature.
- **The Angel of Death (Malak ha-Mavet):** Passes over Egypt killing the firstborn
  (Exodus 12). A terrifying, impersonal executioner of divine will.
- **Watchers (Apocryphal — 1 Enoch):** Angels assigned to watch over humanity who
  instead descended to Earth, mated with human women, and produced the Nephilim.
  Led by Azazel and Semjaza. God sent the Flood partly to destroy their offspring.

### 1C. KEY PATRIARCHS AND PROPHETS

#### ABRAHAM — Father of Nations
- **Role:** Founder of the covenant. Patriarch of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam.
- **Key Stories:**
  - Called by God to leave his homeland and go to an unknown land (Canaan)
  - Promised descendants as numerous as the stars, despite being old and childless
  - The Binding of Isaac (Aqedah) — commanded to sacrifice his son Isaac as a test
    of faith. An angel stops him at the last moment. A ram is provided instead.
  - Bargained with God to spare the city of Sodom if even ten righteous people
    could be found there
- **Worldbuilding Note:** Abraham introduces the concept of covenant — a binding
  agreement between a god and a people that shapes all subsequent events.

#### MOSES — The Lawgiver
- **Role:** Prophet, liberator, lawgiver, mediator between God and Israel
- **Key Stories:** (See Section 4 for full Exodus narrative)
  - Born during Pharaoh's genocide, hidden in a basket on the Nile, raised in
    Pharaoh's own household
  - Encountered God in the Burning Bush; given the divine name "I AM"
  - Led Israel out of Egypt through the Ten Plagues and the parting of the Red Sea
  - Received the Ten Commandments on Mount Sinai amid thunder, fire, and earthquake
  - Wandered the desert for forty years with a rebellious people
  - Forbidden to enter the Promised Land for striking a rock in anger instead of
    speaking to it — a devastating punishment for a minor disobedience
  - Died on Mount Nebo, looking across at the land he could never enter

#### DAVID — The Warrior King
- **Role:** Shepherd boy, giant-slayer, poet (Psalms), king, ancestor of the Messiah
- **Key Stories:** (See Section 6 for full hero treatment)
  - Anointed as a boy by the prophet Samuel
  - Killed the giant Goliath with a sling and stone
  - Beloved companion of Jonathan (Saul's son) — their bond is one of the most
    intense relationships in the Bible
  - Became king of Israel after Saul's death
  - Committed adultery with Bathsheba and arranged her husband Uriah's death in
    battle — confronted by the prophet Nathan
  - His son Absalom rebelled against him
  - "A man after God's own heart" — yet deeply flawed

#### THE MAJOR PROPHETS

- **Isaiah:** Saw God on his throne (Isaiah 6). Spoke of the coming Messiah — the
  "suffering servant." Condemned social injustice. Poetic, visionary, political.
- **Jeremiah:** The "weeping prophet." Called as a youth. Warned of Jerusalem's
  destruction (and was proved right). Imprisoned, mocked, persecuted. Endured.
- **Ezekiel:** Exiled priest. Had the most bizarre and vivid visions in the Bible —
  the valley of dry bones, the wheel within a wheel, the four living creatures,
  the restored Temple. Performance artist — lay on his side for 390 days, cooked
  food over dung, shaved his head with a sword.
- **Daniel:** Exiled to Babylon. Interpreter of dreams. Survived the lion's den.
  His three friends survived the fiery furnace. His apocalyptic visions (Daniel 7-12)
  influenced all subsequent Jewish and Christian eschatology.

### 1D. NEW TESTAMENT FIGURES

#### JESUS OF NAZARETH — The Christ
- **Titles:** Son of God, Son of Man, Messiah (Christ), Lamb of God, King of Kings,
  Word of God (Logos), Emmanuel ("God with us"), the Good Shepherd, the Light of
  the World, the Alpha and the Omega
- **Nature (as described in tradition):** Both fully divine and fully human. Pre-existent
  with God ("In the beginning was the Word" — John 1:1). Born of a virgin.
- **Key Events:**
  - Born in Bethlehem. Visited by shepherds and Magi (wise men).
  - Baptized by John the Baptist in the Jordan River. The Holy Spirit descended as
    a dove; God spoke from heaven.
  - Tempted by Satan in the wilderness for forty days.
  - Called twelve apostles. Taught in parables.
  - Performed miracles: water to wine, walking on water, feeding 5,000, raising the
    dead (Lazarus), healing the blind and leprous, calming storms.
  - The Transfiguration — appeared in dazzling divine glory on a mountain, flanked by
    Moses and Elijah.
  - Entered Jerusalem on a donkey (Palm Sunday). Overturned the money-changers' tables
    in the Temple.
  - The Last Supper — shared bread and wine with his disciples, instituting the
    Eucharist. Washed their feet.
  - Betrayed by Judas Iscariot for thirty pieces of silver. Arrested in Gethsemane.
  - Tried, mocked, scourged, and crucified under Pontius Pilate.
  - Died on the cross. The sky darkened. The Temple veil tore in two.
  - Buried in a tomb. Rose on the third day. Appeared to disciples over forty days.
  - Ascended into Heaven.
- **Worldbuilding Note:** The narrative of Jesus combines the archetype of the dying-
  and-rising god, the suffering servant, the divine king, and the wandering teacher.
  His crucifixion-as-victory (defeat that is actually triumph) is one of the most
  powerful narrative inversions in mythology.

#### THE TWELVE APOSTLES
- **Peter (Simon):** Fisherman. Impulsive, devoted. "The Rock" on which the church
  is built. Denied Jesus three times. Became the leader of the early church.
  Tradition: crucified upside down in Rome.
- **John:** "The beloved disciple." Author attributed to the Gospel of John and
  Revelation. Present at the crucifixion.
- **Judas Iscariot:** The betrayer. Sold Jesus for thirty silver pieces. Hanged
  himself (Matthew) or fell and burst open (Acts). The archetype of the traitor.
- **Paul (Saul of Tarsus):** NOT one of the original twelve. A Pharisee who
  persecuted Christians, then had a dramatic conversion on the road to Damascus
  (blinding light, voice of Jesus). Became Christianity's greatest missionary and
  theologian. Wrote much of the New Testament. Executed in Rome.

#### MARY — The Mother of Jesus
- Conceived Jesus while a virgin through the Holy Spirit
- Present at the crucifixion
- Venerated especially in Catholic and Orthodox traditions
- Her "Magnificat" (Luke 1:46-55) is a revolutionary poem about God overthrowing
  the powerful and lifting the lowly

#### JOHN THE BAPTIST
- Prophet who prepared the way for Jesus
- Lived in the wilderness, ate locusts and wild honey, wore camel hair
- Baptized Jesus in the Jordan
- Beheaded by King Herod at the request of Salome (who danced for his head)

## 2. COSMOLOGY

### 2A. THE STRUCTURE OF CREATION

**The Ancient Hebrew Model (Old Testament):**
- **Shamayim (The Heavens):** A solid dome (firmament/raqia) separating the waters
  above from the waters below. The sun, moon, and stars are set IN the firmament.
  Above the firmament: the waters of heaven. God's throne is above all.
- **Eretz (The Earth):** A flat disc or platform resting on pillars above the waters
  below. The center of creation and God's primary concern.
- **Tehom (The Deep):** Primordial waters beneath and surrounding the earth. Chaos
  waters held back by God's power. Connected to the Flood narrative — the "fountains
  of the great deep" burst forth (Genesis 7:11).
- **Sheol (The Underworld):** Beneath the earth. The realm of the dead. NOT initially
  a place of punishment — simply the destination of ALL the dead, righteous and wicked.
  A shadowy, diminished existence. "The pit."

**The Developed Christian Model (New Testament and later tradition):**
- **Heaven (multiple levels):** Paul mentions being "caught up to the third heaven"
  (2 Corinthians 12:2). Later tradition elaborated this into multiple heavens (seven
  heavens is common in Jewish mysticism and apocryphal texts).
  - First Heaven: The sky/atmosphere
  - Second Heaven: Outer space / the stars
  - Third Heaven: Paradise / God's throne room
  - (Apocryphal traditions expand to seven or ten heavens)
- **Earth:** The mortal realm. The stage of salvation history.
- **Hell / Gehenna:** (Developed from the Valley of Hinnom outside Jerusalem, where
  child sacrifice once occurred.) A place of eternal punishment by fire. "Where the
  worm does not die and the fire is not quenched" (Mark 9:48).
- **Hades:** Greek-influenced term for the abode of the dead. In the parable of
  Lazarus and the Rich Man (Luke 16), Hades contains both a place of torment and
  "Abraham's Bosom" (a place of comfort), separated by an uncrossable chasm.
- **Purgatory (Catholic tradition):** An intermediate state of purification for souls
  destined for Heaven but not yet fully purified. Not directly Biblical but developed
  from inferences in 2 Maccabees and Paul's letters.
- **The New Heaven and New Earth (Revelation 21):** At the end of time, God creates a
  new, perfected creation. The New Jerusalem descends from heaven — a city of gold
  and jewels, with no Temple (because God's presence fills it entirely), no sun (God
  himself is the light), and a river of life flowing through it.

### 2B. THE COSMIC TIMELINE (Linear, Not Cyclical)

Unlike Hindu cosmology, Biblical time is LINEAR — it has a definite beginning, a
narrative arc, and a definite end:

1. **Creation** (Genesis 1-2) — God creates ex nihilo (from nothing)
2. **The Fall** (Genesis 3) — Sin enters through human disobedience
3. **The Covenant** (Genesis 12 onward) — God chooses a people
4. **The Law** (Exodus 20) — God gives commandments
5. **The Prophets** — God warns and guides
6. **The Incarnation** (Gospels) — God enters creation as a human
7. **The Church Age** (Acts onward) — The message spreads
8. **The End Times** (Revelation) — Judgment, destruction, renewal
9. **The New Creation** (Revelation 21-22) — Eternal paradise, no more death

**Worldbuilding Note:** Linear time creates urgency that cyclical time does not.
Every moment matters because it will not recur. History is moving toward a climax.
This structure generates a fundamentally different kind of narrative tension.

## 3. CREATION MYTHS

### 3A. Genesis 1 — The Seven-Day Creation (The Priestly Account)

**Day 1:** God creates light and separates it from darkness. "Let there be light."
Creation by SPEECH — the word is the instrument of creation.
**Day 2:** God creates the firmament (raqia), separating the waters above from the
waters below. The sky is a solid dome.
**Day 3:** God gathers the waters below, revealing dry land. Plants and trees appear.
**Day 4:** God creates the sun, moon, and stars — set in the firmament. (Note: light
exists before the sun — light is a property of God, not the sun.)
**Day 5:** God creates sea creatures and birds.
**Day 6:** God creates land animals. Then: "Let us make humanity in our image" (the
plural "us" is endlessly debated). Male and female created simultaneously.
**Day 7:** God rests. The Sabbath is established as cosmic principle.

**Key Feature:** Order from chaos. Separation and naming as acts of power. Creation
through language. Hierarchy and structure. Goodness declared at each stage.

### 3B. Genesis 2 — The Garden (The Yahwist Account)

- God forms Adam from the dust of the ground (adamah) and breathes life into him.
- Plants the Garden of Eden with the Tree of Life and the Tree of the Knowledge
  of Good and Evil.
- Places Adam in Eden. Commands him not to eat from the Tree of Knowledge.
- Creates animals and brings them to Adam to name (naming = authority/dominion).
- No suitable companion found. God causes a deep sleep, takes a rib, creates Eve.
- Adam and Eve are naked and unashamed.

### 3C. The Fall (Genesis 3)

- The Serpent (the most cunning of all creatures) persuades Eve that eating the
  forbidden fruit will make her "like God, knowing good and evil."
- Eve eats. Adam eats. Their eyes are opened — they realize their nakedness.
- They hide from God. God finds them. They blame each other and the Serpent.
- **Consequences:**
  - The Serpent is cursed to crawl on its belly
  - Eve: pain in childbirth, subordination
  - Adam: cursed ground, toil, death ("dust you are, and to dust you shall return")
  - Both are expelled from Eden. Cherubim with flaming swords guard the way back.
- **Worldbuilding Note:** The Fall introduces the concept of a LOST paradise — a
  perfect state that can never be regained. This longing for a lost golden age is
  one of the most powerful emotional engines in worldbuilding.

### 3D. Apocryphal Creation Material

- **2 Enoch (Book of the Secrets of Enoch):** God existed in light before creation.
  He called forth Adoil, a great light, which burst apart to reveal the visible
  creation. He called forth Archas from the darkness to create the lower things.
- **The Wisdom of Solomon:** Wisdom (personified as a woman) was present at creation
  as God's partner/craftsman.

## 4. MAJOR MYTHS AND STORIES

### 4A. THE FLOOD (Genesis 6-9)

- Humanity has become wicked. God regrets creating them.
- The "sons of God" (angels? — see Nephilim below) have mated with human women.
- God commands Noah, the one righteous man, to build an ark.
- Noah builds the ark, gathers animals (two of each, or seven of clean animals).
- The Flood: rain for forty days and nights; the fountains of the deep burst open.
- All life outside the ark perishes.
- The ark rests on Mount Ararat. Noah sends a raven, then a dove (which returns
  with an olive branch).
- God makes a covenant with Noah: never again to destroy the earth by flood.
  The rainbow is the sign of this covenant.
- **Worldbuilding Note:** The Flood as divine reset — destroying a failed creation
  but preserving a remnant — is a powerful narrative template.

### 4B. THE TOWER OF BABEL (Genesis 11)

- Humanity speaks one language. They build a tower to reach heaven.
- God descends to see it and says: "If they can do this, nothing will be impossible
  for them." (A remarkable admission of human potential as threatening.)
- God confuses their languages and scatters them across the earth.
- **Worldbuilding Note:** This myth explains linguistic diversity as divine
  intervention against human unity — an unusual portrayal of God as actively limiting
  human potential.

### 4C. THE EXODUS

**Full Narrative Arc:**
1. The Israelites are enslaved in Egypt for generations.
2. Pharaoh orders the killing of Hebrew male infants.
3. Moses is hidden, floated down the Nile, and raised by Pharaoh's daughter.
4. Moses kills an Egyptian, flees to Midian, becomes a shepherd.
5. God appears in the Burning Bush. Reveals the name "I AM." Commands Moses to
   free the Israelites.
6. Moses confronts Pharaoh: "Let my people go."
7. **The Ten Plagues:**
   - Water to blood
   - Frogs
   - Gnats/lice
   - Flies
   - Livestock disease
   - Boils
   - Hail
   - Locusts
   - Three days of darkness
   - Death of the firstborn (the Passover — lamb's blood on doorposts protects)
8. Pharaoh relents. The Israelites flee. Pharaoh changes his mind and pursues.
9. **The Parting of the Red Sea** — Moses stretches out his staff; the waters divide;
   Israel crosses on dry ground; the waters close on the Egyptian army.
10. Forty years of wandering in the wilderness: manna from heaven, water from rock,
    quail from the sky, the golden calf apostasy.
11. **Mount Sinai:** God descends in fire, smoke, and earthquake. Gives the Ten
    Commandments and the Law. Moses's face glows after speaking with God.
12. Moses dies on Mount Nebo. Joshua leads the people into the Promised Land.

### 4D. THE BOOK OF JOB

- Job is blameless and righteous. God boasts of him to Satan.
- Satan argues Job is only righteous because God has blessed him. "Remove the
  blessings, and he will curse you."
- God permits Satan to test Job. Job loses his wealth, his children, and his health.
- Three friends argue he must have sinned (the theology of deserved suffering).
- Job demands an answer from God.
- **God answers from the whirlwind** — but does NOT explain. Instead, God delivers
  a litany of cosmic power: "Where were you when I laid the foundations of the earth?
  Can you bind the chains of the Pleiades? Do you give the horse its strength?"
- Job submits: "I had heard of you by the hearing of the ear, but now my eye sees you."
- God restores Job's fortunes — but the dead children are not the same children.
- **Worldbuilding Note:** Job is the most philosophically sophisticated text in the
  Bible. It demolishes simple moral causation. God is not a vending machine of justice.
  The universe is not fair, and God refuses to explain why. For worldbuilders, this
  suggests that the most compelling divine figures are those who REFUSE to justify
  themselves.

### 4E. THE BOOK OF REVELATION

The final book of the Bible. An apocalyptic vision received by John on the island
of Patmos. Dense with symbolism, terrifying imagery, and cosmic warfare.

**Key Elements:**
- **Letters to Seven Churches:** Warnings and encouragements.
- **The Throne Room of Heaven:** God on a throne of jasper and carnelian, surrounded
  by twenty-four elders, four living creatures (lion, ox, human, eagle), and a sea
  of glass.
- **The Seven Seals:** Opened by the Lamb (Christ). Each releases a judgment:
  - Four Horsemen of the Apocalypse: Conquest (white horse), War (red horse),
    Famine (black horse), Death (pale horse)
  - Earthquakes, cosmic signs, the wrath of God
- **The Seven Trumpets:** Each sounds a worse catastrophe — hail and fire, a burning
  mountain cast into the sea, a star (Wormwood) poisoning the waters, darkened skies.
- **The Two Witnesses:** Prophets who breathe fire, stop the rain, turn water to
  blood. Killed by the Beast, lie dead for three days, then resurrect and ascend.
- **The Woman and the Dragon:** A woman clothed in the sun gives birth to a male
  child. A great red dragon (Satan) with seven heads tries to devour the child. The
  child is caught up to God. War in Heaven — Michael casts the dragon down.
- **The Beast (Antichrist):** Rises from the sea. Seven heads, ten horns. Given
  authority for forty-two months. Forces all to receive the "mark of the beast" — the
  number 666.
- **The Second Beast (False Prophet):** Rises from the earth. Performs miracles.
  Enforces worship of the first Beast.
- **Babylon the Great:** A woman riding a scarlet beast. Represents corrupt worldly
  power. "Fallen, fallen is Babylon the great!"
- **The Battle of Armageddon:** The forces of evil gather. Christ returns on a white
  horse, with a sword proceeding from his mouth. The Beast and False Prophet are
  thrown into the Lake of Fire.
- **The Millennium:** Satan is bound for 1,000 years. The righteous reign with Christ.
- **The Final Judgment:** Satan is released, deceives the nations, is defeated and
  thrown into the Lake of Fire. The dead are raised and judged from the Book of Life.
  Death and Hades are thrown into the Lake of Fire.
- **The New Heaven and New Earth:** God creates everything anew. The New Jerusalem
  descends. "God will wipe every tear from their eyes. There will be no more death."

### 4F. OTHER SIGNIFICANT NARRATIVES

- **Cain and Abel:** The first murder. Brother kills brother. "Am I my brother's
  keeper?" Cain is cursed to wander but protected by a divine mark.
- **Jacob and Esau:** Twins in conflict from the womb. Jacob steals Esau's birthright
  and blessing. Later wrestles with an angel (or God) all night and is renamed Israel
  ("he who struggles with God"). Walks with a limp ever after.
- **Joseph:** Sold into slavery by his brothers. Rises to become vizier of Egypt
  through his gift of dream interpretation. Saves his family during famine. "You
  meant it for evil, but God meant it for good."
- **Elijah:** Prophet of fire. Called down fire from heaven on Mount Carmel to defeat
  the prophets of Baal. Fled into the wilderness in despair. Heard God in the "still
  small voice." Ascended to heaven in a chariot of fire without dying.
- **Jonah:** Sent to preach repentance to Nineveh. Fled. Swallowed by a great fish
  for three days. Obeyed. Nineveh repented. Jonah was ANGRY that God showed mercy to
  his enemies. God rebuked him.
- **Solomon:** Son of David. Wisest man who ever lived. Built the First Temple. Asked
  for wisdom instead of wealth (God gave both). Wrote Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, Song
  of Songs. Fell into idolatry through his many foreign wives.

## 5. CREATURES AND MONSTERS

### LEVIATHAN
- **Description:** A massive sea serpent/dragon. Multi-headed (Psalm 74:14). Breathes
  fire (Job 41). Impervious to weapons. Its scales are shields. The sea boils in
  its wake.
- **Origin:** Possibly connected to Canaanite chaos monster Lotan/Litanu. Represents
  primordial chaos.
- **Key Texts:** Job 41 (extensive description), Psalm 74, Isaiah 27
- **Significance:** God "plays with" Leviathan (Psalm 104:26) — demonstrating absolute
  mastery over chaos. Will be slain at the end of time and its flesh served as a feast.

### BEHEMOTH
- **Description:** An enormous land creature. Eats grass like an ox. Its tail "sways
  like a cedar." Bones like bronze, limbs like iron. Drinks rivers.
- **Key Text:** Job 40:15-24
- **Significance:** Paired with Leviathan as examples of God's creative power beyond
  human comprehension. Sometimes identified with a hippopotamus, elephant, or
  sauropod — or understood as a mythological creature.

### ZIZ (Apocryphal)
- A giant bird that completes the trio with Leviathan (sea) and Behemoth (land).
  Its wings block the sun. It will also be served at the messianic feast.

### NEPHILIM
- **Description:** "The Nephilim were on the earth in those days — and also afterward —
  when the sons of God went to the daughters of humans and had children by them. They
  were the heroes of old, men of renown." (Genesis 6:4)
- **Origin:** Offspring of fallen angels (or "sons of God") and human women.
- **Nature:** Giants. Mighty warriors. Associated with the pre-Flood world's corruption.
- **Apocryphal Detail (1 Enoch):** The Watchers (fallen angels led by Semjaza) taught
  humanity forbidden knowledge — metalworking, cosmetics, astrology, warfare. Their
  Nephilim offspring were insatiable giants who devoured humanity's resources and
  eventually each other.
- **Post-Flood:** Giants appear again (Anakim, Rephaim) — Goliath may be a descendant.

### SERAPHIM (as creatures)
- Six-winged serpentine beings of fire. They attend God's throne. Their cry of "Holy,
  Holy, Holy" shakes the Temple foundations. One touches Isaiah's lips with a burning
  coal to purify him.

### THE FOUR LIVING CREATURES (Ezekiel / Revelation)
- Four beings with four faces each (human, lion, ox, eagle). Covered in eyes. Six
  wings (in Revelation). They surround God's throne and never cease worship.

### DEMONS
- In the Old Testament: "shedim" (foreign gods), "seirim" (goat-demons), evil spirits
  sent by God (1 Samuel 16 — an evil spirit from the Lord torments Saul).
- In the New Testament: unclean spirits that possess humans. Jesus casts them out
  regularly. "Legion" — a man possessed by thousands of demons, which Jesus casts into
  a herd of pigs.
- **Apocryphal (Testament of Solomon):** Solomon commands demons to build the Temple,
  binding them with a ring given by the archangel Michael. Each demon has a name,
  a specialty, and a weakness.
- **Asmodeus:** A demon of lust/wrath in the Book of Tobit. Killed seven of Sarah's
  husbands on their wedding nights. Driven away by the smoke of fish organs.
- **Azazel:** In 1 Enoch, a leader of the fallen Watchers. Taught humans to make
  weapons and cosmetics. Bound and cast into darkness until the Day of Judgment.
  In Leviticus 16, the scapegoat is sent to Azazel in the wilderness.
- **Lilith (Jewish tradition):** Adam's first wife (before Eve) in some rabbinic
  texts. Refused to be subordinate. Became a night demon who threatens infants.
  Not in the canonical Bible but deeply embedded in Jewish folklore.

## 6. HEROES AND FIGURES

### MOSES — The Liberator
- See Section 1C and 4C. The archetype of the reluctant prophet. Stuttered. Argued
  with God. Led a nation. Never reached the destination.

### DAVID — The Flawed King
- Shepherd, musician, warrior, poet, king, adulterer, murderer (by proxy), penitent.
- Killed Goliath as a youth with a sling stone — the iconic underdog victory.
- Played the harp to soothe King Saul's madness; Saul later tried to kill him.
- Spent years as a fugitive, gathering a band of outlaws and outcasts.
- Became Israel's greatest king. Unified the tribes. Conquered Jerusalem.
- His affair with Bathsheba and murder of Uriah represent his deepest failure — yet
  his repentance (Psalm 51) became the model for all contrition.
- **Worldbuilding Note:** David's greatness is inseparable from his failure. He is
  the template for the hero-king who is also profoundly, destructively human.

### SAMSON — The Tragic Strongman
- A Nazirite (consecrated to God from birth — no wine, no haircut, no corpse contact).
- Endowed with supernatural strength by the Spirit of God.
- Killed a lion with bare hands. Slew a thousand Philistines with a donkey's jawbone.
  Tore a city gate off its hinges.
- His weakness: women. Specifically, Delilah.
- Delilah extracted his secret (his strength was in his hair/his vow). She cut his
  hair while he slept. The Philistines captured him, blinded him, and enslaved him.
- His final act: his hair regrew. At a Philistine celebration, he pushed apart the
  pillars of the temple, killing himself and thousands. "Let me die with the
  Philistines."
- **Worldbuilding Note:** Samson is the anti-hero — divinely empowered but personally
  undisciplined. His strength is a gift, not an achievement, and it is taken away
  when his vow is broken.

### ELIJAH — The Prophet of Fire
- Called down fire from heaven to consume a sacrifice, defeating 450 prophets of Baal.
- Confronted kings (Ahab) and queens (Jezebel).
- Fed by ravens. Multiplied a widow's oil and flour.
- After his greatest victory, fled into the desert in despair and begged to die.
  God fed him and met him in silence.
- Ascended to heaven alive in a chariot of fire. Expected to return before the
  Messiah (Malachi 4:5).

### JOSHUA — The Conqueror
- Moses's successor. Led Israel into the Promised Land.
- The walls of Jericho fell when the Israelites marched around them seven times
  and blew trumpets.
- Commanded the sun to stand still during battle (Joshua 10:12-13).

### ESTHER — The Hidden Queen
- Jewish woman who became queen of Persia by concealing her identity.
- When the vizier Haman plotted genocide against the Jews, Esther revealed her
  identity and intervened. "If I perish, I perish."
- Haman was hanged on the gallows he built for Esther's cousin Mordecai.
- God is never mentioned in the Book of Esther — the only Biblical book where
  this is true. Divine providence operates entirely through human action.

### RUTH — The Loyal Foreigner
- A Moabite woman who chose to follow her Israelite mother-in-law Naomi after
  both their husbands died. "Where you go, I will go."
- Became an ancestor of King David and, by extension, of Jesus.

### JOSEPH OF GENESIS — The Dreamer
- See Section 4F. Betrayed by brothers, enslaved, imprisoned, and elevated to power
  through dream interpretation. A story of providence working through suffering.

## 7. THE AFTERLIFE

### 7A. SHEOL (Old Testament)
- The abode of ALL the dead — righteous and wicked alike.
- Not a place of punishment or reward. A shadowy existence.
- The dead are "shades" (rephaim) — diminished, silent, cut off from God.
- "The dead do not praise the Lord" (Psalm 115:17).
- Over time, a distinction develops between areas of Sheol for the righteous
  and the wicked.

### 7B. HEAVEN
- The dwelling of God and the angels.
- In later Jewish and Christian thought, the destination of the righteous dead.
- Described variously: God's throne room, a paradise, a garden, a city (New Jerusalem).
- Jesus: "In my Father's house are many rooms" (John 14:2).
- Paul: "To be absent from the body is to be present with the Lord" (2 Cor. 5:8).
- Revelation 21-22: The New Jerusalem — gold, jewels, river of life, tree of life
  bearing twelve kinds of fruit, no more tears, no more death, God's face visible.

### 7C. HELL / GEHENNA
- **Gehenna:** Named after the Valley of Hinnom — a real valley outside Jerusalem
  associated with child sacrifice (to Molech) and later used as a garbage dump
  with perpetual fires.
- Jesus used Gehenna as a metaphor for divine judgment: "where the worm does not die
  and the fire is not quenched."
- **Lake of Fire (Revelation):** The final destination of Satan, the Beast, the False
  Prophet, Death, Hades, and all whose names are not in the Book of Life.
  "The second death."
- **Theological debate:** Eternal conscious torment vs. annihilation vs. universal
  reconciliation — all three views have had advocates throughout Christian history.

### 7D. PURGATORY (Catholic/Orthodox)
- An intermediate state of purification.
- Supported by 2 Maccabees 12:46 (prayer for the dead) and inferences from Paul.
- Not a "lesser hell" but a process of preparation for the full presence of God.

### 7E. THE RESURRECTION
- A central Biblical concept: the dead will be raised physically at the end of time.
- Not just spiritual survival but BODILY resurrection — transformed, glorified bodies.
- Jesus's resurrection is the prototype: physical (he ate, was touched) but also
  transformed (appeared in locked rooms, was not always recognized).
- "The last enemy to be destroyed is death" (1 Corinthians 15:26).

### 7F. THE LAST JUDGMENT
- All humanity raised and judged.
- **The Great White Throne (Revelation 20):** God sits on a white throne. Books are
  opened. The Book of Life is opened. The dead are judged by what is written.
- **The Sheep and the Goats (Matthew 25):** Jesus separates the righteous from the
  wicked based on how they treated "the least of these" — the hungry, the sick, the
  imprisoned, the stranger.

**Worldbuilding Note:** The Biblical afterlife differs fundamentally from the Hindu
model. It is PERMANENT (no reincarnation), BODILY (not just spiritual), and FINAL
(one judgment, one destination). This creates a very different narrative weight —
every life is a one-shot, irreversible story.

## 8. SACRED PLACES

- **The Garden of Eden:** The original paradise. Located "eastward" with four rivers
  (Pishon, Gihon, Tigris, Euphrates). Guarded by cherubim and a flaming sword.
  Lost to humanity.
- **Mount Sinai (Horeb):** Where God gave the Law. Covered in fire, smoke, and
  thunder. The mountain of theophany.
- **The Tabernacle:** A portable sanctuary carried through the wilderness. Designed
  according to God's exact specifications. Contained the Ark of the Covenant, the
  menorah, the altar of incense, and the showbread. The Holy of Holies was separated
  by a veil — only the High Priest could enter, once a year.
- **The Temple of Solomon (First Temple):** Built in Jerusalem. The permanent house
  of God. When the Ark was placed inside, the Glory of God filled the Temple so
  intensely that the priests could not stand. Destroyed by Babylon in 586 BCE.
- **The Second Temple:** Rebuilt after exile. Expanded by Herod the Great. Destroyed
  by Rome in 70 CE. Its Western Wall remains.
- **Jerusalem:** The Holy City. "City of David." "The city where God has chosen to
  place his name." Scene of the crucifixion and resurrection. The New Jerusalem
  will descend from heaven at the end of time.
- **Bethlehem:** Birthplace of David and Jesus. "O little town of Bethlehem."
- **Mount Ararat:** Where Noah's ark rested after the Flood.
- **The Jordan River:** Boundary of the Promised Land. Site of Jesus's baptism.
  Naaman was healed of leprosy by washing in it seven times.
- **Babylon:** The great enemy city. Site of the Exile. Symbol of worldly corruption
  and pride. "By the rivers of Babylon, we sat and wept" (Psalm 137).
- **Egypt:** The house of bondage. Symbol of slavery and oppression. Also a place of
  refuge (the Holy Family fled there).
- **Golgotha (Calvary):** "Place of the skull." Where Jesus was crucified.
- **Patmos:** Island where John received the Revelation.
- **The Wilderness:** Not a specific place but a recurring sacred space — where
  Israel wandered, where Elijah despaired, where Jesus was tempted. The place of
  testing, deprivation, and encounter with God.

## 9. ARTIFACTS AND ITEMS

### THE ARK OF THE COVENANT
- A gold-covered wooden chest containing the stone tablets of the Ten Commandments,
  Aaron's rod (that budded), and a jar of manna.
- Topped with two golden cherubim whose wings form God's "mercy seat" — the place
  where God's presence dwells on Earth.
- Carried before Israel in battle. Its presence caused the walls of Jericho to fall.
- When the Philistines captured it, plagues followed until they returned it.
- Uzzah touched it to steady it and was struck dead — it is THAT holy.
- Placed in Solomon's Temple. Its fate after the Temple's destruction is unknown.
- **Worldbuilding Note:** An object so holy it kills the unauthorized who touch it.
  The ultimate sacred artifact — powerful, dangerous, and eventually LOST.

### THE HOLY GRAIL (Medieval Christian tradition)
- The cup used by Jesus at the Last Supper, and/or the vessel that caught his blood
  at the crucifixion.
- Not Biblical but deeply embedded in Christian mythology through Arthurian legend.
- The quest for the Grail represents the ultimate spiritual journey.
- Only the pure of heart can find it (Galahad, not Lancelot).

### OTHER ARTIFACTS
- **The Staff of Moses:** Turned into a serpent. Parted the Red Sea. Struck the rock
  for water. The instrument of multiple miracles.
- **Aaron's Rod:** Budded and blossomed overnight to confirm Aaron's priesthood.
  Kept in the Ark.
- **The Bronze Serpent (Nehushtan):** Moses crafted a bronze serpent on a pole; anyone
  bitten by a serpent who looked at it was healed. Later destroyed by King Hezekiah
  because people were worshipping it.
- **The Urim and Thummim:** Mysterious objects kept in the High Priest's breastplate,
  used to determine God's will. Their exact nature is unknown — possibly stones
  used for casting lots.
- **The Tablets of Stone:** The Ten Commandments, "written by the finger of God."
  Moses smashed the first set in anger at the golden calf; God wrote a second set.
- **The Manna:** Bread from heaven. Appeared each morning as white flakes, tasting
  like "wafers made with honey." Could not be stored overnight (it rotted) except
  on the Sabbath.
- **Elijah's Mantle:** His cloak. When Elijah ascended, it fell to Elisha, who struck
  the Jordan and parted it — confirming the transfer of prophetic power.
- **Goliath's Sword:** After David killed Goliath, he kept the giant's sword. Later
  retrieved it from the priests at Nob.
- **The Nails of the Crucifixion / The True Cross / The Crown of Thorns / The
  Spear of Longinus (Holy Lance):** Relics associated with the crucifixion. The spear
  that pierced Jesus's side (John 19:34) became a powerful relic in medieval tradition.
- **The Thirty Pieces of Silver:** Judas's payment for betrayal. He threw them back
  into the Temple before hanging himself. Used to buy a potter's field.

## 10. CULTURAL PRACTICES

### WORSHIP AND PRAYER
- **The Sabbath:** Seventh day of rest. From sundown Friday to sundown Saturday.
  Modeled on God's rest after creation. A defining practice of Judaism.
- **Synagogue Worship:** Torah readings, prayer, psalms, teaching. Developed during
  and after the Babylonian Exile when the Temple was destroyed.
- **Temple Worship (historical):** Animal sacrifice, incense, priestly rituals.
  The High Priest entered the Holy of Holies once a year (Yom Kippur) with the blood
  of a bull and goat.
- **Christian Worship:** Eucharist/Communion (bread and wine as the body and blood
  of Christ), prayer, hymns, preaching, baptism.
- **The Lord's Prayer:** "Our Father, who art in heaven..." — the model prayer taught
  by Jesus.
- **Psalms:** 150 poems/songs covering the full range of human emotion — praise,
  lament, fury, despair, joy, gratitude, vengeance.

### SACRIFICE
- **Animal Sacrifice:** Central to Old Testament worship. Burnt offerings, sin
  offerings, peace offerings, guilt offerings. The blood is the key element — "the
  life is in the blood" (Leviticus 17:11).
- **The Passover Lamb:** Slaughtered and its blood applied to doorposts. Central
  memorial of liberation from Egypt.
- **The Scapegoat (Yom Kippur):** Two goats — one sacrificed, one loaded with the
  people's sins and driven into the wilderness "for Azazel."
- **The Crucifixion as Sacrifice:** Christian theology interprets Jesus's death as the
  ultimate sacrifice — "the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world."

### FESTIVALS AND HOLY DAYS
- **Passover (Pesach):** Commemorates the Exodus. Seder meal, unleavened bread, bitter
  herbs, retelling of the story.
- **Yom Kippur (Day of Atonement):** The holiest day. Fasting, repentance, the
  scapegoat ritual.
- **Sukkot (Feast of Tabernacles):** Commemorates the wilderness wandering. Temporary
  booths are built.
- **Purim:** Celebrates Esther's deliverance. Reading of the Book of Esther. Joy and
  feasting.
- **Hanukkah:** Rededication of the Temple after the Maccabean revolt. The oil that
  lasted eight days.
- **Easter:** Resurrection of Jesus. The central Christian holy day.
- **Christmas:** Birth of Jesus (date is traditional, not historical).
- **Pentecost:** The descent of the Holy Spirit on the apostles. They spoke in
  tongues. Considered the "birthday" of the Church.

### COVENANT
- The binding agreement between God and his people.
- **Covenants in sequence:** Noah (rainbow — never again a flood), Abraham (land,
  descendants, blessing), Moses (the Law — obedience for blessing), David (eternal
  throne), Jesus (the "new covenant" in his blood).
- Covenant is THE structuring principle of Biblical narrative. Every major event
  relates to making, breaking, or renewing covenant.

### PROPHECY
- Prophets are God's spokespeople. Not primarily fortune-tellers but truth-tellers —
  they diagnose the present more than they predict the future.
- The prophetic pattern: God sends a warning. The people ignore it. Judgment falls.
  A remnant survives. Hope is restored.
- Prophets are often social critics — condemning injustice, greed, and the oppression
  of the poor as much as (or more than) religious failure.

## 11. THEMES AND WORLDBUILDING LESSONS

### COVENANT AS NARRATIVE ENGINE
- The entire Biblical narrative is structured around covenants — promises made, kept,
  broken, and renewed.
- **Lesson:** Building your world around a binding agreement between a divine power
  and a people creates automatic narrative tension. What happens when the people
  fail? What happens when the god seems to fail?

### THE PATTERN OF EXILE AND RETURN
- Eden (paradise) > Fall (exile from paradise) > Covenant (promise of return) >
  Exodus (liberation) > Promised Land (partial return) > Exile to Babylon (loss
  again) > Return > Destruction of Temple (loss again) > Hope of ultimate restoration.
- **Lesson:** The cycle of loss and partial restoration, with the promise of final
  redemption, creates powerful narrative momentum across millennia of story.

### LINEAR TIME AND ESCHATOLOGY
- Biblical time moves toward a CLIMAX. History has a direction and a purpose.
  Every event matters because it contributes to an ultimate resolution.
- **Lesson:** If your world has a prophesied end, every generation lives in
  anticipation. Characters ask: "Are we the ones?" This creates urgency that
  cyclical cosmologies do not.

### THE UNDERDOG AND THE REMNANT
- God consistently chooses the weak, the small, the unlikely: the youngest son
  (David), the barren woman (Sarah), the stuttering fugitive (Moses), the enslaved
  nation (Israel), the virgin peasant girl (Mary), the crucified carpenter (Jesus).
- **Lesson:** A world where divine power works through weakness, not strength, inverts
  all conventional power dynamics.

### THE FLAWED HERO
- Biblical heroes are deeply flawed: David murders, Moses rages, Peter denies,
  Paul persecutes. Yet they are not rejected — they are redeemed, used, and
  celebrated alongside their failures.
- **Lesson:** Flawed heroes are more compelling than perfect ones. The Bible never
  pretends its heroes are flawless.

### JUSTICE VS. MERCY
- The central tension of the Biblical narrative. God is perfectly just AND perfectly
  merciful. These two attributes are in constant, productive tension.
- Jonah is ANGRY that God shows mercy to Nineveh. The older brother in the Prodigal
  Son parable is ANGRY that the father celebrates the wastrel's return.
- **Lesson:** A world where justice and mercy genuinely conflict — where both are
  GOOD and yet they pull in opposite directions — generates the deepest moral drama.

### THE POWER OF NAMING AND SPEAKING
- God creates by speaking. Adam names the animals (and thus has authority over them).
  Knowing God's name (YHWH) is a form of intimacy. Jacob is renamed Israel after
  wrestling with God. Abram becomes Abraham. Saul becomes Paul.
- **Lesson:** If words have power — if naming something gives you authority over it,
  if speaking creates reality — then language itself becomes a form of magic.

### APOCALYPTIC IMAGINATION
- Revelation demonstrates that ultimate evil can be defeated, but the cost is
  staggering. The world must be UNMADE before it can be remade. Victory involves
  passing through catastrophe, not avoiding it.
- **Lesson:** A worldbuilding tradition that ends in apocalypse and renewal provides
  the ultimate narrative stakes — and the promise that destruction is not meaningless.

### TYPOLOGY AND FORESHADOWING
- The Bible constantly echoes itself. Isaac carrying the wood for his own sacrifice
  prefigures Jesus carrying the cross. The bronze serpent in the wilderness prefigures
  the crucifixion. The Passover lamb prefigures the Lamb of God. Jonah's three days
  in the fish prefigure the three days in the tomb.
- **Lesson:** A world with deep internal echoes — where later events mirror earlier
  ones, where prophecy is fulfilled in unexpected ways — feels profoundly unified
  and intentional.

### THE WILDERNESS AS CRUCIBLE
- Major Biblical figures are forged in wilderness: Moses (40 years), Israel (40 years),
  Elijah (40 days), Jesus (40 days), David (years as a fugitive).
- **Lesson:** The place of deprivation is also the place of transformation. Removing
  comfort forces encounter with the divine.
